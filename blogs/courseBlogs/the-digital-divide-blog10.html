The Digital Divide

Written by Devan Gray - Wednesday, June 3, 2020

What is Digital Divide?

In recent times the interaction between peoples, computers and the internet has seen an increase in information and growth throughout the world. The need for the ability to access this wealth of information has become vastly important for everyone in order to keep up with these current times of rapid technological innovations.

The term “Digital Divide” refers the issue around the differing amount of information or access to information between individuals or countries who have access to computers and/or the internet such as the wealthy, middle-class, and young portion of the population and those underprivileged who do not have access; such as the poor, rural, elderly, and handicapped portion of the population.

A Few Factors causing the Digital Divide and its Impact

Geographical Restrictions

Less developed countries with weaker economies lack technology and infrastructure that prevents them from setting up high-speed internet connections for its population. Some areas lack a strong infrastructure more then others which contributes to the divide. “In-country geographical restrictions also widen the digital divide. Urban regions are more likely to have access to 4G or fibre optic internet than rural or mountainous zones.” (Steele, 2020)

Education

The hugely different levels of education between individuals widen the digital inequality gap. “College degree holders are perceived to be 10X more likely to tap into the full potential of the internet and computers in their day to day lives compared to individuals with high school education or lower.” (Steele, 2020)

Due to the divide some educational places cannot provide its students with a rich library of information and therefore the learning of advanced skills, academic success and excellent scientific research cannot take place.

Income

In direct connection with education, the differing levels of household incomes across countries and areas have a huge effect on widening the digital gap. For the low-income population money is put towards basic essentials such as food, water, and housing foremost where technology such as computers and the internet is seen as a luxury. “High-income earners ($75,000 – R1,2mil) are 20X more likely to access the internet than low-income earners ($30,000 – R500k).” (Steele, 2020)

Telecommunication services get a much-needed boost to the economic growth of countries, where wide use of internet and technology provides productiveness within a country. With the divide this is made difficult.
